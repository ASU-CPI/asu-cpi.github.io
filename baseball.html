Research Proposal

Crowdsourcing the Hall of Fame Vote: A Research Proposal

We propose an open governance research experiment that would test the theory behind the “Wisdom of Crowds”: that under the right circumstances - i.e., diversity of opinion, independence in deciding, decentralization and aggregation - groups are remarkably intelligent, even smarter than the smartest people in them, and that even if most of the people within a group are not well-informed and rational, the group can still reach a collectively wise decision.[1]

A research setting that provides an ideal opportunity to test this theory is the annual vote to induct retired baseball players into that sport’s Hall of Fame. Every year in late November, when the list of candidate names is publicly released, ballots containing the alphabetized names of candidate players are mailed to the roughly 600 voting members of the Baseball Writers’ Association of America (BBWAA). Voters are able to select up to ten names (and as few as none) deemed worthy of election, returning their ballot by December 31. Any candidate receiving votes equalling or exceeding 75% of the ballots returned is then deemed elected to the Hall.

The current method can be called an expert panel approach: active and honorary members of the BBWAA, people whose profession involves writing about baseball in newspapers. Since this process continues regardless of whether this research agenda is implemented, we have the opportunity to compare that expert process to a crowdsourced approach open to any interested voter with an Internet connection. We propose offering an Internet-based voting process that would mirror in detail and timeframe the annual Hall of Fame vote, differing only in respect of the eligible voters (closed - as in the case of the BBWAA - vs. open), and the medium (paper - in the BBWAA case - vs. Internet-based).

The central research question would be a comparison of the outcomes from the two processes; if the BBWAA expert panel process is argued to be a wise decision of professional baseball writers, does a similar wisdom emerge from a crowdsourced approach? Given that the four conditions for a wise crowd result seem to exist in this case - diversity of opinion, independence in deciding, decentralization and aggregation - should the results match, this would confirm the theory underlying the wisdom of crowds (a preliminary result suggests this may be true). Should the results diverge, the research project would use additional data and analysis to investigate whether the four conditions were indeed true, or whether the wisdom of crowds theory needs to be reconsidered in this case.

In addition to this basic, core question, the research would also explore questions specific to how members of the BBWAA and “the crowd” evaluate “Hall-worthiness”, and general questions about open governance, preferential voting methods for selecting amongst alternatives and the comparison of human judgement to algorithmic approaches to rank-ordering players.

Implementation

1. Online Ballot

A prototype online ballot based on the 2013 BBWAA process is provided as an example. Using the survey software provider Qualtrics, a robust platform for facilitating Internet-based voting by large numbers of voters is easily managed. Qualtrics includes safeguards against “ballot-stuffing” and the survey is easily integrated within an existing website. This prototype ballot has a maximum of five questions and a minimum of three:

  1. A list of candidate names, each of which is hyperlinked to their official bio hosted by the Baseball Hall of Fame where voters can read a narrative on the player’s career and summary statistics. The survey allows the user to select between 0 and 10 names (this question matches the voting process of the BBWAA).
  2. An option to rank-order the names selected (yes / no response). Selecting yes takes the user to question 2.a; selecting no takes the user to question 3.
  1. Rank ordering: If yes is selected to question 2, the names selected there are imported into question 2.a where the user can drag-and-drop the names into their preferred order. This ordering will facilitate subsequent analysis outside of the core question that compares the crowd’s judgement to that of the BBWAA members.
  1. All users are presented with the invitation to participate in a user exit survey. Selecting no completes the survey and submits the user’s accumulated responses.
  1. Survey: Selecting yes takes the user to an exit survey that would collect a number of variables that can be used to model the factors that relate to the user’s selections.

2. Partnerships

  • Media partnership (e.g., ESPN; Cronkite School) to increase visibility and legitimacy
  • Support from the Baseball Hall of Fame and Baseball Writers’ Association of America.
  • Collaboration with journalism and sports academics.

3. Timing

Launch to coincide with the fall 2014 balloting (late November 2014). Voting results announced to coincide with official BBWAA vote (early January 2015). Research reports to follow. We anticipate running the process in subsequent years.

4. Research Questions

Core question: how does a crowdsourced approach compared to an expert panel approach?

Supplemental questions: how are player careers evaluated; comparison of human judgement to algorithmic approaches; modelling how players are evaluated / what influences judgement; how does preferential voting (and various counting algorithms) change the outcomes?

5. Funding

Given the quick turnaround, and the low anticipated overhead in conducting this research, no grant proposal is envisioned in this first year.
